Treatment Policies


HIV Treatment Around the World

It is approximated that 38 million people across the world are living with HIV, the virus that causes AIDS. The first cases were reported in 1981 and about 76 million people have become infected since then. One of the Sustainable Development Goals set out by the United Nations is to end the AIDS epidemic by 2030.


While there is no effective cure for HIV, antiretroviral therapy (ART) suppresses the virus and reduces both the progression of the disease and onward transmission, which has led to a decrease in both death and infection rates.



Tuberculosis Treatment Around the World


Around 10 million people get tuberculosis every year and more than 4 million of them are not diagnosed and treated. We have in fact had a cure for tuberculosis since 1948, yet the disease is responsible for 2 million deaths annually and is now the biggest infectious killer of adults worldwide. In 1993, the World Health Organization started promoting a treatment policy called DOTS, which was branded as very cost-effective.


While DOTS reduced the number of tuberculosis cases in sub-Saharan countries, it never eliminated the disease. This was largely due to it being a policy solely focused on treatment and not prevention, which was considered “too expensive” in poor countries.
